This article from the American Society of Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine provides a clinical overview of neuroplastic pain — what it is, how to diagnose it, and how to treat it.

**What they covered:**

- Neuroplastic pain (also called nociplastic pain or primary pain) is pain driven by altered central nervous system processing, not ongoing tissue damage
- It involves three overlapping concepts: nociplastic pain (the formal IASP term), central sensitization (the mechanism), and neuroplastic pain (the brain-change perspective)
- Diagnosis is clinical — no lab test or scan can confirm it. Key features include widespread pain, hyperalgesia, allodynia, and disproportionate pain responses
- Brain imaging shows that fear, anxiety, and emotion circuits get tightly coupled with pain circuits

**Why it matters:**

The article emphasizes something crucial: patient education is not just informational — it's therapeutic. Understanding the mechanism of your pain is the first step toward recovery. The same neuroplasticity that keeps pain going can be harnessed to turn it off.

For clinicians, the article recommends a 5-step approach: validate the patient's experience, explain that pain is always generated by the nervous system, use illustrative examples (phantom limb pain, rubber hand illusion), commit to supporting the patient, and connect them with evidence-based pain science resources.
